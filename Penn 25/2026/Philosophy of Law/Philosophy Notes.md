## Hart, *The Concept of Law*, Chapter 4
- All legal systems can be boiled down to a sovereign and its subjects.
- There exist "habits" (see below) and "continuity", that is, the persistence and consistency of laws after the party which invented them is no longer here.
	- **Habits** are when people follow a behavior that is not legally binding. For instance, people queuing quietly on a bus.
	- **Rules** build off of this by adding binding consequences for those who detract from such habits.
	- Acceptance of rules constitutes Hart's idea of the "internal point of view".
	- **Obedience** is a regular pattern of compliance that is derived from external forces (fear of sanctions or exercise of force). There is no appeal to standards or generalized behavior.
- Austin's theory requires that the sovereign is above the law.
- Hart wants to challenge this notion through illustrating that law can exist without a supreme lawmaker.

What are the roles that habits play?
- They are naturally perceived to be behaviors, but in a legal context, this is different - come back to this later
- Successive considerations - habitual obedience to one sovereign does not guarantee that it will persist when the body of that sovereign is transferred or continued through another party.
- Necessitates laws surrounding *succession*. But the existence of succession raises some important concerns and complications to the basic concept of imperative theory, namely in the difference between habits and rules.


Hart uses this new definition of habits to challenge the imperative theory, 

> [!NOTE] Quote
> Even if we concede that a person, such as Rex, whose general orders are habitually obeyed, may be called a legislator and his orders laws, habitats of obedience to each succession of such legislators are not enough to account for the *right* of a successor to succeed and for the consequent continuity in legislative power. First, because habits are not 'normative', they cannot confer rights or authority on anyone. Secondly, because habits of obedience to one individual cannot, though accepted rules can, refer to a class or line of future successive legislators as well as to the current legislator, or render obedience to them likely. So the fact that there is habitual obedience to one legislator neither affords grounds for the statement that his successor has the right to make law, nor for the factual statement that he is likely to be obeyed. (116/390)



The next step is to understand how law persists over an even longer period of time:
- Laws persist temporally because they continue to be recognized by the present sovereign.
- This is a tacit expression of the sovereign's will.
